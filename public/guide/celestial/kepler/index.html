      Kepler's Laws

      Newton’s law of universal gravitation describes gravity as a force by stating that every particle attracts every other particle in the universe with a force that is proportional to the product of their masses and inversely proportional to the square of the distance between their centers of mass. Consider two bodies of masses m1m_1m1​ and m2m_2m2​, with position vectors r1⃗\vec{r_1}r1​​ and r2⃗\vec{r_2}r2​​. The force exerted on body 2 due to body 1 is

      F⃗=−Gm1m2∣r2⃗−r1⃗∣3(r2⃗−r1⃗) \vec{F} = -\frac{G m_1 m_2}{|\vec{r_2} - \vec{r_1}|^3} (\vec{r_2} - \vec{r_1}) F=−∣r2​​−r1​​∣3Gm1​m2​​(r2​​−r1​​)

      We define the following:

      • Separation vector: r⃗=r2⃗−r1⃗ \vec{r} = \vec{r_2} - \vec{r_1} r=r2​​−r1​​
      • Total mass: M=m1+m2M = m_1 + m_2M=m1​+m2​
      • Reduced mass: m=m1m2m1+m2m = \frac{m_1 m_2}{m_1 + m_2}m=m1​+m2​m1​m2​​
      • Gravitational parameter: μ=GM\mu = GMμ=GM

      Here G is the gravitational constant, equal to 6.67430×10−11 m3 kg−1 s−26.67430 \times 10^{-11} \: \mathrm{m^3\,kg^{-1}\,s^{-2}}6.67430×10−11m3kg−1s−2 . From hereon, we will study the trajectory of the separation vector r⃗\vec{r}r. Using Newton’s second law F⃗=ma⃗\vec{F} = m \vec{a}F=ma, we can write

      r⃗¨=−μr⃗r3 \ddot{\vec{r}} = -\mu \frac{\vec{r}}{r^3} r¨=−μr3r​

      Working in the center of mass frame, we have

      m1r1⃗+m2r2⃗=0⃗ m_1 \vec{r_1} + m_2 \vec{r_2} = \vec{0} m1​r1​​+m2​r2​​=0

      Hence the individual position vectors can be expressed as

      r1⃗=−m2Mr⃗ ,r2⃗=m1Mr⃗ \vec{r_1} = \frac{-m_2}{M} \vec{r} \,, \qquad \qquad \vec{r_2} = \frac{m_1}{M} \vec{r} r1​​=M−m2​​r,r2​​=Mm1​​r

      In the limit m1≫m2m_1 \gg m_2m1​≫m2​, we get that

      • M=m1M = m_1M=m1​
      • m=m2m = m_2m=m2​
      • r1⃗=0\vec{r_1} = 0r1​​=0
      • r2⃗=r⃗\vec{r_2} = \vec{r}r2​​=r

      We see that the trajectory of the separation vector is simply the trajectory of the smaller mass, m2m_2m2​, in the center of mass frame, while the larger mass m1m_1m1​ is at rest.

      Constants of Motion

      The total orbital angular momentum is given by

      L⃗=m1r1⃗×v1⃗+m2r2⃗×v2⃗=mr⃗×v⃗ \begin{align*} \vec{L} &= m_1 \vec{r_1} \times \vec{v_1} + m_2 \vec{r_2} \times \vec{v_2}\\ &= m \vec{r} \times \vec{v} \end{align*} L​=m1​r1​​×v1​​+m2​r2​​×v2​​=mr×v​

      where v⃗=r⃗˙\vec{v} = \dot{\vec{r}}v=r˙. The total energy of the system is given by

      E=12m1v12+12m2v22−Gm1m2∣r1⃗−r2⃗∣2=12mv2−μmr2 \begin{align*} E &= \frac{1}{2} m_1 v_1^2 + \frac{1}{2} m_2 v_2^2 - \frac{G m_1 m_2}{|\vec{r_1} - \vec{r_2}|^2} \\ &= \frac{1}{2} m v^2 - \mu \frac{m}{r^2} \end{align*} E​=21​m1​v12​+21​m2​v22​−∣r1​​−r2​​∣2Gm1​m2​​=21​mv2−μr2m​​

      We define the specific angular momentum and specific energy as

      h⃗=L⃗m=r⃗×r⃗˙ε=Em=12v2−μr \begin{align*} \vec{h} &= \frac{\vec{L}}{m} = \vec{r} \times \dot{\vec{r}}\\ \tag{3.1.1} \varepsilon &= \frac{E}{m} = \frac{1}{2} v^2 - \frac{\mu}{r} \end{align*} hε​=mL​=r×r˙=mE​=21​v2−rμ​​(3.1.1)​

      Since gravitation is a central force, the angular momentum is conserved. Moreover since it is conservative, the total energy is also conserved. Hence h⃗\vec{h}h and ε\varepsilonε are constants of motion.

      Since h⃗⋅r⃗=0\vec{h} \cdot \vec{r} = 0h⋅r=0, h⃗\vec{h}h lies perpendicular to the plane of motion, and hence defines the plane of motion. We have

      ddt(h⃗×r⃗˙)=h⃗×r⃗¨=(r⃗×r⃗˙)×(−μr⃗r3)=−μ1r3[(r⃗⋅r⃗)r⃗˙−(r⃗⋅r⃗˙)×r⃗]=−μ(r⃗˙r−r⃗r˙r2)=ddt(−μr⃗r) \begin{align*} \frac{d}{dt} (\vec{h} \times \dot{\vec{r}}) = \vec{h} \times \ddot{\vec{r}} &= (\vec{r} \times \dot{\vec{r}}) \times \left( -\mu \frac{\vec{r}}{r^3} \right) \\ &= -\mu \frac{1}{r^3} \left[(\vec{r} \cdot \vec{r}) \dot{\vec{r}} - (\vec{r} \cdot \dot{\vec{r}}) \times \vec{r} \right] \\ &= -\mu (\frac{\dot{\vec{r}}}{r} - \vec{r} \frac{\dot{r}}{r^2}) = \frac{d}{dt} \left(-\mu \frac{\vec{r}}{r} \right) \end{align*} dtd​(h×r˙)=h×r¨​=(r×r˙)×(−μr3r​)=−μr31​[(r⋅r)r˙−(r⋅r˙)×r]=−μ(rr˙​−rr2r˙​)=dtd​(−μrr​)​  ⟹  ddt(h⃗×r⃗˙+μr⃗r)=0⃗ \implies \frac{d}{dt} \left( \vec{h} \times \dot{\vec{r}} + \mu \frac{\vec{r}}{r} \right) = \vec{0} ⟹dtd​(h×r˙+μrr​)=0

      Hence we define the eccentricity vector as

      e⃗=−h⃗×r⃗˙μ−r⃗r(3.1.2)\tag{3.1.2} \vec{e} = -\frac{\vec{h} \times \dot{\vec{r}}}{\mu} - \frac{\vec{r}}{r} e=−μh×r˙​−rr​(3.1.2)

      This is another constant of motion. It lies in the plane of motion, hence h⃗⋅e⃗=0\vec{h} \cdot \vec{e} = 0h⋅e=0. It points in the direction of the periapsis. The mangnitude of the eccentricity vector is

      e=1+2εh2μ2(3.1.3)\tag{3.1.3} e = \sqrt{1 + \frac{2 \varepsilon h^2}{\mu^2}} e=1+μ22εh2​​(3.1.3)

      First Law

      The angle between the eccentricity vector and the position vector is called the mean anamoly θ\thetaθ

      r⃗⋅e⃗=recos⁡θ=r⃗⋅(−h⃗×r⃗˙μ−r⃗r)=h2μ−r \vec{r} \cdot \vec{e} = r e \cos \theta = \vec{r} \cdot \left( -\frac{\vec{h} \times \dot{\vec{r}}}{\mu} - \frac{\vec{r}}{r} \right) = \frac{h^2}{\mu} - rr⋅e=recosθ=r⋅(−μh×r˙​−rr​)=μh2​−r  ⟹  r=h2/μ1+ecos⁡θ(3.1.4)\tag{3.1.4} \implies \boxed{r = \frac{h^2 / \mu}{1 + e \cos \theta}} ⟹r=1+ecosθh2/μ​​(3.1.4)

      This, if one may recognize, is the equation of a conic section in polar coordinates, with the focus being at origin and length of the semi latus rectum being p=h2μp = \frac{h^2}{\mu}p=μh2​, which gives the relation

      h=μp(3.1.5)\tag{3.1.5} h = \sqrt{\mu p}h=μp​(3.1.5)

      The magnitude of e⃗\vec{e}e determines the shape of the trajectory:

      • e=0e = 0e=0: Circle
      • 0≤e<10 \leq e < 10≤e<1: Ellipse
      • e=1e = 1e=1: Parabola
      • e>1e > 1e>1: Hyperbola

      where the focus of the conic section lies at the center of mass of the system.

      The individual trajectories of the two bodies in the center of mass frame is therefore

      r1=m2Mh2/μ1+ecos⁡θ ,r2=m1Mh2/μ1+ecos⁡θ r_1 = \frac{m_2}{M} \frac{h^2 / \mu}{1 + e \cos \theta} \,, \qquad \qquad r_2 = \frac{m_1}{M} \frac{h^2 / \mu}{1 + e \cos \theta} r1​=Mm2​​1+ecosθh2/μ​,r2​=Mm1​​1+ecosθh2/μ​

      The trajectories of the two bodies however will be oriented opposite to each other, with an angle of π\piπ radian between them, keeping the center of mass fixed.

      Another trajectory is possible, which is a straight line. This is the case where h⃗=0⃗\vec{h} = \vec{0}h=0, and the bodies are moving directly towards each other. The eccentricity of such a trajectory is 111. However, depending on the total energy of the system, the trajectory can be classified as:

      • ε<0\varepsilon < 0ε<0: radial elliptic trajectory
      • ε=0\varepsilon = 0ε=0: radial parabolic trajectory
      • ε>0\varepsilon > 0ε>0: radial hyperbolic trajectory

      The energy of the system can thus be determined by just the eccentricity eee and length of semi latus rectum ppp of the trajectory. The total energy of the system is given by

      e2=1+2hεμ2  ⟹  ε=12(e2−1)μ2h2e^2 = 1 + \frac{2h \varepsilon}{\mu^2} \implies \varepsilon = \frac{1}{2} \left( e^2 - 1 \right) \frac{\mu^2}{h^2}e2=1+μ22hε​⟹ε=21​(e2−1)h2μ2​  ⟹  ε=−12μ(1−e2)p(3.1.6)\tag{3.1.6} \implies \varepsilon = -\frac{1}{2} \frac{\mu (1 - e^2)}{p}⟹ε=−21​pμ(1−e2)​(3.1.6)

      We see that the sign of the total energy depends on the eccentricity of the trajectory

      • 0≤e<10 \leq e < 10≤e<1: An elliptical trajectory gives a negative total energy, and hence a bound orbit
      • e=1e = 1e=1: A parabolic trajectory gives a zero total energy, and hence an unbound orbit
      • e>1e > 1e>1: A hyperbolic trajectory gives a positive total energy, and hence an unbound orbit

      We see that all bound orbits are ellipses, and all unbound orbits are hyperbolae or parabolae.

      For our solar system, the sun is much more massive than any of the planets, hence is nearly at the center of mass, or the focus of the elliptical orbits of all planets. This proves Kepler’s first law of planetary motion, which states that the orbit of a planet is an ellipse with the sun at one of the foci.

      Second Law

      The cross product of two vectors gives the area of a parallelogram possessing sides of those vectors, hence the triangular area dAdAdA swept out in a short period of time is given by half the cross product of the r⃗\vec{r}r and dx⃗\vec{dx}dx vectors, for some short piece of the orbit, dxdxdx.

      dA=12∣r⃗×dx⃗∣=12∣r⃗×v⃗dt∣=h2dt dA = \frac{1}{2}|\vec{r} \times \vec{dx}| = \frac{1}{2}|\vec{r} \times \vec{v} dt| = \frac{h}{2} dt dA=21​∣r×dx∣=21​∣r×vdt∣=2h​dt  ⟹  dAdt=h2(3.1.7)\tag{3.1.7} \implies \boxed{\frac{dA}{dt} = \frac{h}{2}} ⟹dtdA​=2h​​(3.1.7)

      which is constant. This proves Kepler’s second law of planetary motion, which states that a line segment joining a planet and the sun sweeps out equal areas during equal intervals of time.

      Third Law

      Consider two bodies moving in bound elliptical orbits, with the semi-major axis of the orbit being aaa, and the semi-minor axis being bbb. The lengths of the two axes are related by the eccentricity of the orbit, eee, as

      e2=1−b2a2 e^2 = 1 - \frac{b^2}{a^2} e2=1−a2b2​

      The area of an ellipse is πab=πa21−e2\pi a b = \pi a^2 \sqrt{1 - e^2}πab=πa21−e2​. Moreover, the length of the semi latus rectum is p=b2a=a(1−e2)p = \frac{b^2}{a} = a (1 - e^2)p=ab2​=a(1−e2), hence h=μa(1−e2)h = \sqrt{\mu a (1 - e^2)}h=μa(1−e2)​

      Integrating the equation of areal velocity, we have

      dA=h2dt∫dA=h2∫dtπa21−e2=12μa(1−e2)P \begin{align*} dA &= \frac{h}{2} dt\\ \int dA &= \frac{h}{2} \int dt\\ \pi a^2 \sqrt{1 - e^2} &= \frac{1}{2} \sqrt{\mu a (1 - e^2)} P\\ \end{align*} dA∫dAπa21−e2​​=2h​dt=2h​∫dt=21​μa(1−e2)​P​

      Rearranging, we get

      P2=4π2μa3(3.1.8)\tag{3.1.8} \boxed{P^2 = \frac{4 \pi^2}{\mu} a^3} P2=μ4π2​a3​(3.1.8)

      This is known as Newton’s form of the third law. If we work in the units of AU, sidereal years, and solar masses, we have GM⊙=4πG M_\odot = 4 \piGM⊙​=4π. Hence the equation simplifies to

      a3=MP2(3.1.9)\tag{3.1.9} \boxed{a^3 = M P^2} a3=MP2​(3.1.9)

      In our solar system, M=M⊙+mplanet≈M⊙=1M = M_\odot + m_{planet} \approx M_\odot = 1M=M⊙​+mplanet​≈M⊙​=1, we get the relation P2=a3P^2 = a^3P2=a3. This proves Kepler’s third law of planetary motion, which states that the square of the period of revolution of a planet is directly proportional to the cube of the semi-major axis of its orbit.
